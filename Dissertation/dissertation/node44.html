Dynamic Frequency/Channel Assignment

<#1591#>DFA<#1591#> is a channel allocation scheme where frequencies assigned to cells are not permanent but rather assigned to cells as the need arises[#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###]. Therefore all the frequencies licensed by a particular network are available to each and every cell to establish a communication link as long as the channel does not violate the co-channel reuse constraint [#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###].

The co-channel reuse constraint must be adhered to otherwise the amount of interference occurring on the communication link will be too much[#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###]. This constraint forms part of the electromagnetic constraints, which are described in section~#sec:Interference#1595>.

The <#1596#>DFA<#1596#> allocation scheme is ideally suited for micro cellular wireless networks. The traffic on these networks have the characteristic of being immensely unpredictable as traffic demand varies constantly[#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###,#MobileWirelessCommunications##1###].

As the name indicates, micro cellular wireless networks have much smaller cell sizes than macro cellular networks. Thus a cell in a micro cellular network must handle a lot more handover requests than a cell in a macro cellular network, since an <#1598#>MS<#1598#> with an active connection is much more likely to move out of the coverage area of a micro cell than a macro cell [#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###,#WirelessCommunications##1###].

Since a micro cellular network has increased handover requests between cells compared with a macro cellular network, a <#1600#>DFA<#1600#> scheme must rapidly allocate frequencies to requesting cells that must handle the handovers[#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###,#WirelessCommunications##1###].

<#1602#>DFA<#1602#> is much more efficient than <#1603#>FFA<#1603#> when the amount of mobile traffic on the network is relatively low[#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###,#WirelessCommunications##1###]. On the other hand, when the network is under heavy mobile traffic load, the <#1605#>FFA<#1605#> scheme outperforms the <#1606#>DFA<#1606#> scheme[#WirelessCommunications##1###]. The <#1608#>DFA<#1608#> shceme is outperformed because it allocates frequencies to cells in an inefficient arrangement that might affect the amount of interference encountered on the network[#MobileWirelessCommunications##1###].

Finally <#1610#>DFA<#1610#> inherently requires a great deal more computational power than <#1611#>FFA<#1611#>, since the frequencies need to be selected and allocated with great speed, otherwise the cell requesting a channel will not be able to handle the call and will therefore block the call or drop the call[#PrinciplesMobileCommunication##1###,#WirelessCommunications##1###].

This concludes the discussion on the different allocation schemes used in modern cellular networks. In the next section a description is given of what interference is and why it is important for cellular networks. An overview will also be given of when interference occurs.
