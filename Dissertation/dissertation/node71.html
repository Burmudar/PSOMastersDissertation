Introduction

<#2481#>TS<#2481#> was first proposed by Glover[#Glover89##1###] as a new searching technique to help algorithms avoid getting trapped in local optima in combinatorial and optimisation problems [#TabuRCAProblem##1###]. Since Glover introduced the algorithm in the 1980s, tabu search has been applied to a wide range of problems such as the vehicle routing problem[#TabuVechicleRoutingWithTimeWindows##1###], <#2485#>FAP<#2485#>[#TabuMontemanniSmith##1###], capacitated-lot sizing problem[#TabuCarryOver##1###], Nurse Scheduling[#TabuNurse##1###] and the Resource Constrained Assignment Problem[#TabuRCAProblem##1###].

Even though the problems mentioned differ by a large margin, the algorithm has been successful in most optimisation problems it has been applied to. If one observes the results obtained in research [#TabuMontemanniSmith##1###,#tabuglobalplanning3g##1###], it can be inferred that tabu search has on average obtained the best results compared with previous attempts with other algorithms.

<#2491#>TS<#2491#> resembles in its most basic form the hill-climbing search algorithm[#TabuBiddingStrats##1###]. The hill-climbing search algorithm starts from an initial solution and then iteratively moves from the current solution to a neighbouring solution[#AIModernApproach##1###]. Each neighbour is rated based on its attractiveness as a possible optimal solution that the algorithm is being applied to[#AIModernApproach##1###].

The hill-climbing algorithm moves to the neighbour with the highest rating without considering whether the neighbour might lead the algorithm astray, to a position where the neighbours are in fact worse than previously encountered possible solutions[#AIModernApproach##1###].

The <#2497#>TS<#2497#> algorithm addresses this shortcoming by introducing the concept of memory[#TabuBiddingStrats##1###]. The memory of the algorithm is actually a history of previous solutions that the algorithm has moved to in its search for the search space for a solution[#TabuBiddingStrats##1###].

General search algorithms like hill-climbing, random-restart4.2 or scatter search tend to get trapped in local optima [#AIModernApproach##1###]. The local optima might be a very attractive solution and thus general search algorithms will not move to better solutions since, according to the algorithm's built-in strategy, it has found the best solution.

In actual fact the solution that was found is the best solution in the local search space, but not in the global search space[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###,#AIModernApproach##1###]. Therefore an important characteristic of algorithms being applied to optimisation problems is breaking out of local optima[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###,#AIModernApproach##1###].
