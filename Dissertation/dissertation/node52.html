Philadelphia Benchmarks

The Philadelphia benchmarks are derived from an instance that was introduced in 1973 by Anderson[#AndersonPhiladelphia##1###]. Each instance is a hexagonal grid of cells that overlaps the area of interest. At the centre of each cell there is a transmitter. Past approaches used these hexagonal systems to model modern cellular networks [#Karen2004##1###,#ExactMIFAP##1###].

In this benchmark interference is measured by a co-channel reuse distance[#Karen2004##1###]. This distance stipulates that the difference between the frequencies assigned to two cells must be greater than or equal to a certain value #tex2html_wrap_inline15069# mathend000#. A channel cannot be assigned to a cell if it violates this minimum distance [#ExactMIFAP##1###].

These benchmarks are typically used to test algorithms developed for <#2024#>MS-FAP<#2024#>, since there is no concept of cost or penalty for interference incurred by violating constraints.
