Mutation Operator

The mutation operator is a probabilistic operator and is applied to individials in the offspring population with a probability referred to as the mutation rate[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###]. The purpose of the mutation operator is to increase the diversity of the genes of a individual chromosomes[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###]. By introducing new genes into an individual the diversity of the populations characteristics are increased[#CoactiveFuzzyGA##1###,#AcceleratingGA##1###,#ConstrainedGA##1###].

The mutation operator has no previous information on the chromosome it is mutating; thus it is entirely possible that the mutation may modify the chromosome for the worse [#AcceleratingGA##1###]. A worse solution might lead the algorithm out of local optima or lead it down a new path to find the global optima, but this is not always the case [#AdaptiveSAGA##1###,#FamilyGA##1###,#ConstrainedGA##1###]. It is for this reason that it is recommended that the mutation rate be set to a low value to ensure good solutions are not distored too much[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###].

In a survey done by Engelbrecht[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###] another mutation operator is discussed. Instead of mutating a small part of randomly selected chromosomes, this operator generates new offspring to be inserted back into the population. The operator randomly generates a new chromosome and then uses any of the previously discussed crossover operators (see page~#sec:crossover#2978>).

The mutation operator is not always a basic random replacement of genes operation. In research done by Il-kwon Jeong and Ju-jang Lee[#AdaptiveSAGA##1###], a mutation operator is presented that uses the <#2980#>SA<#2980#> algorithm to determine the genes that need to be replaced. The <#2981#>SA<#2981#> mutation operator generates a new chromosome from which genes are used to replace in the chromosome being mutated [#AdaptiveSAGA##1###]. Addition mutation operators are discussed in Engelbrecht[#CompuIntelligenceIntro##1###].
